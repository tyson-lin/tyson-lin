BIRDS Lab
November 2021 - Present
I work under Professor Shai Revzen at the Biologically Inspired Robotics and Dynamical Systems (BIRDS) Lab. The main area of study of our lab is legged locomotion.

My first project was to 3D motion capture data on our hopping robot using Qualysis. The math involved in this is beyond me, but the experiment required each leg of the robot to have a different spring constant. Because of that, the robot did not hop straight up, and hopped in a completely random direction in each hop, and therefore would often hop out of the motion capture system. I spent a lot of time optimizing the gait so that it hops inside the motion capture system.

For the past year I have been working with another undergraduate student on a quadcopter (I know that quadcopters don't have legs, but I think the project is really fun and I am learning a lot). We want to study how mechanical design parameters can be chosen through iteration, through the lens of quadcopter motor positions. So we're going to fly a quadcopter, have a computer analyze the flight and give us new parameters, and repeat with a new chassis.

So far, I've designed the paremterized chassis and brought up the hardware. The challenge now is to generalize a controller for any set of motor positions. I've built an inverted stewart platform to help with this. With springs instead of hydraulics, it acts as a 6 degree-of-freedom force/torque sensor that lets us quickly calculate the torques and forces each motor applies to the chassis as a function of thrust.

As of January 2023, I don't have any time to work on the project. There is a new team of students that will be working on the quadcopter, and I'll be helping them understand what we've done, and what generally needs to happen.

Hands on Robotics
August 2022 - December 2022
I worked as an instructional aide (IA) for EECS 464 - Hands on Robotics (HRB). HRB is a senior/graduate ECE elective, where students work in teams to design robots to accomplish various tasks. The class uses Robotis DYNAMIXEL motors, and the primary building material is foamcore, so that students can quickly prototype mechanical designs. The last project of the course is to design and build a robot arm that can draw a square on an easel with any orientation.

Students are also introduced to a wide range of topics including particle filters, PID control, and forward/inverse kinematics. Technical writing through project reports is also a big focus throughout the course.

I had two primary responsibilities as an IA. I had to make sure that each teams hardware (motors and harnessing) was properly working, and that they had enough. I also worked with each team to help them laser cut and 3D print parts as needed.
